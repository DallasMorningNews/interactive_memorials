Dallas’ hidden history

For too long the history of Dallas white majority culture has muted the stories of those who really built Dallas' culture

Published March 7, 2017

    It’s been said that the winners write the histories of any given place, and there is some truth to that. In Dallas, like most places, that has meant that the majority culture — white, Christian, southern — has held the pen of history for all this generations who followed John Neely Bryan and this city’s earliest settlers.

    We see the markers to that history all over town, in the names of our buildings and our schools and streets, and in the statutes, memorials and other testaments to the past. These physical reminders of our past speak through the ages, and will continue to speak to future generations, about what went into the making of this place, and what values those who have lived here, who do live here, cherish most.

    That’s as it should be, but the story these reminders tell is a narrow one. For in Dallas, as in most places, the true history of how we got to where we are now is made up of a 100 smaller histories, of peoples and movements and events that defined not just the majority culture, but all the myriad colors and backgrounds and perspectives of the people who have lived here. Those subtler stories, the ones so often missing from the dominant histories of the city, are vital to understanding the city.

    Today The Dallas Morning News launches an effort to flesh out the histories of Dallas by inviting our readers from every background to help us map the hidden histories of their own communities within Dallas. We want to mark the places where events happened that helped shape the history of people and groups in Dallas whose stories have largely been left out of the story of Dallas.
